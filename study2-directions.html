That ‘Seems’ Fair

Vicarious Opportunity Cost and Transaction Fairness Judgments

01

Background — Study 1 / Pilot

The pilot asked two questions about how buyers read a seller's forgone alternatives, and whether that reading moves what they call a fair price.

RQ1

Are there differences in how we process the opportunity cost of transaction partners?

RQ2

How do these opportunity-cost perception differences impact fair-price judgements?

02

Findings

The four findings below are the premise for the two directions that follow. Each opens for the full result.

Design Study 1 (2×2 between-subjects, N = 233) manipulated relationship type (communal vs. exchange) and seller opportunity cost (package kept vs. discarded), and measured perceived seller opportunity cost, fair price, and perceived worth of the item to the seller.

Click an idea to read more!

When seller opportunity cost was high, communal buyers perceived significantly higher seller opportunity cost than exchange buyers. When it was low, the relationship types did not significantly differ from one another. Both relationship types, however, raised their OC perceptions when moving from the low to the high OC condition.

When the seller's forgone $100 return is made salient, only communal buyers significantly revise their fair-price judgements upward. Exchange buyers do engage in vicarious OC assessments — but those OC perceptions do not create a significant difference in their fair-price assessments.

Keeping the package increased fair price both directly and indirectly, through heightened OC perceptions. This indirect pathway was amplified for communal buyers.

For communal buyers, OC perceptions were raised via the perceived worth of the item to the seller — a serial pathway (package → worth → OC perception → fair price; indirect effect 0.32, CI [0.05, 0.82]). For exchange buyers, the worth pathway was not significant (0.15, CI [−0.10, 0.50]).

Path I · Direction

Does vicarious opportunity-cost neglect exist?

Previous literature on opportunity-cost neglect approached the construct in the first person. Our research questions introduce a new way of approaching opportunity-cost assessments — vicarious opportunity cost. The first step should be identifying whether vicarious opportunity-cost neglect exists at all. We have some evidence on the conditions under which vicarious OC perceptions impact price assessments, but no evidence yet on whether vicarious opportunity-cost neglect exists as a construct.

Study idea A · scenario prompts

High OC

Your friend has a blender. They have a buyer offering $100 for it, and they mention they can sell it to you instead.

Low OC

Your friend has a blender. They haven't found a buyer for it on the marketplace, and they mention they can sell it to you.

Baseline

Your friend has a blender listed on the marketplace, and they mention they can sell it to you.

Study idea B · reflection manipulation

Ask participants to reflect on the seller's opportunity cost, to see whether reflection creates a difference. Same scenario, two prompts:

01one asks for the unfair price; 02the other asks what else the seller could do with the item rather than selling it to you.

While these studies are separate from the relationship-norm differences we first tested in Study 1, they could surface a new contribution more prominently. But since our findings suggest there may be processing differences in how opportunity cost is actually used — exchange conditions running an "I see the opportunity cost immediately, but it's irrelevant" route, while communal conditions run an "I only assess opportunity cost through relational judgements, such as the worth of the item to the person" route — there may be a need to investigate that difference further.

Path II · Direction

Ways of assessing a partner's OC, and the route to price

Idea 1 · Memory categories

Opportunity-cost consideration depends on the accessibility of foregone alternatives within an activated category structure. Spiller suggests that category structures in memory determine first-person opportunity-cost assessments — alternatives that are more typical members of an activated resource-use category are more likely to be retrieved and considered (Spiller, 2011).

We propose that, when opportunity costs are vicarious, accessibility depends not only on the category structure surrounding the resource, but also on the relational schema through which the resource holder is represented.

Relationship schemas organize seller information into different memory categories, altering which representations become accessible as opportunity costs. Exchange schemas organize partner information around inputs and market-related alternatives, allowing observable foregone alternatives to map directly onto opportunity cost.

Communal schemas organize partner information around welfare and subjective value, such that the partner's perceived worth serves as a bridging representation through which a foregone alternative is recategorized as an economic cost. Consequently, identical partner outcomes may enter opportunity-cost judgements through different cognitive routes, depending on which relationship schema is active.

Investigating this framework would take two steps.

Experiment 1 · categorization speed

After activating a communal or exchange schema, we present potential opportunity costs and measure how quickly participants classify each as relevant to the seller's OC. An IAT-like setup asks participants to sort concerns as relevant or not relevant to what the seller might be giving up.
